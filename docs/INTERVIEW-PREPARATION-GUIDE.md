### **Example 3: "Explain 3-way matching and its importance"**

**What It Is:**
- Compares Invoice against PO (quantity, price) and GR (receipt confirmation)
- System blocks if variance exceeds tolerance (typically Â±2-5%)

**Why It Matters:**
1. **Prevents overpayment**: Vendor can't invoice for more than ordered
2. **Catches errors**: Identifies qty discrepancies, pricing mistakes
3. **Fraud prevention**: Ensures goods were actually received before payment
4. **Efficiency**: Reduces manual review and exception handling

**The Flow:**
1. PO created for 100 units @ $10 = $1,000
2. Goods receipt confirms 95 units arrived (variance!)
3. Vendor invoices for 100 units @ $10 = $1,000
4. System blocks: "GR shows 95 units but invoice is 100"
5. Requires manual review: Call vendor, resolve discrepancy

**KPI Impact:**
- Accuracy: >95% of invoices match without exception
- Cost: Matching done automatically (not manual review)
- Fraud: Eliminates many fraud schemes

### **Example 4: "How do you optimize payment terms?"**

**Current State:**
- Paying vendors in 30 days on average
- DPO (Days Payable Outstanding) = 30 days

**Analysis:**
- Which vendors could pay in 60 days? (lower risk, established suppliers)
- Which offer early pay discounts? (2/10 Net 30 = 36.5% ROI!)

**Strategy:**
1. **Extend with key vendors**: Negotiate 60 days for loyal, stable vendors
   - Benefit: Improves DPO from 30 to 40 days = 10 days cash freed up
   - Risk: Must maintain relationships, suppliers may increase prices
   
2. **Take early pay discounts** when cash allows:
   - 2% off to pay in 10 days vs. 30 days
   - ROI: 2% Ã— (365 Ã· 20) = 36.5% annualized
   - Worth it if we have liquidity
   
3. **Supply chain financing**:
   - Vendor can sell invoice to bank at 95% value
   - Buyer still pays on day 60 per agreement
   - Vendor gets early cash without us paying

**Impact on Cash Flow:**
- If we have $1M monthly spend, extending DPO by 10 days = $333K cash freed up!

### **Example 5: "What's the difference between budget and forecast?"**

**Budget:**
- **What**: Annual plan of what we intend to achieve
- **When**: Set once per year (e.g., in June for next fiscal year)
- **Flexibility**: Fixed for the year (or quarterly updates)
- **Use**: Accountability, assess performance against plan
- **Example**: "We budgeted $100M revenue for the year"

**Forecast:**
- **What**: Realistic expectation of what we'll actually achieve
- **When**: Updated monthly or quarterly
- **Flexibility**: Flexible, updated frequently with actuals
- **Use**: Decision-making, identify issues early
- **Example**: "Q1 actual $25M + Q2 outlook = expecting $95M, not $100M"

**Rolling Forecast:**
- **Hybrid**: Maintains 12-month forward view always
- **Example**: In February, forecast Feb 2026 - Jan 2027
- **Better**: More agile than static annual budget
- **Growing in popularity**: Especially in fast-moving industries

**In Practice:**
- Budget variance (Actual vs. Budget): Tells you planning was wrong
- Forecast variance (Actual vs. Forecast): Tells you current execution is off
- Forecast accuracy: Better metric to monitor (>80% target)
