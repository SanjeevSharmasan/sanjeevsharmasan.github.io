Navigating Your First GCC Role: From Day 1 to High Impact

  • The Three Pillars of Success: Master the Role + Build Relationships + Deliver Visible Impact
  • Mistakes to Avoid: The top performers know what NOT to do as much as what to do

The Critical First 90 Days

Your first quarter in a GCC is not like a traditional job. The pace is different. The expectations are different. And the opportunities to make an impression‚Äîboth positive and negative‚Äîare amplified.

I've hired and mentored hundreds of professionals entering GCC roles. I've seen people skyrocket into senior positions within 3-4 years. I've also seen talented, capable professionals plateau because they didn't navigate their early months strategically.

The difference? Not always capability. Often, it's approach.

The Master Plan: Your First 90 Days

Days 1-15: The Learning Phase

Goal: Understand the ecosystem, not prove yourself yet.

Week 1: Onboarding & Observation
  • Complete all formal onboarding activities
  • Meet everyone - manager, peers, cross-functional teams
  • Ask lots of questions. Everyone expects this from new hires.
  • Document the organizational structure, key processes, recent initiatives
Week 2: Deep Dive Into Your Role
  • Shadow experienced team members for full days
  • Run through 2-3 complete transaction cycles
  • Document the process flow, pain points, system navigation
  • Identify what slows you down or confuses you
Week 3: Clarify Expectations
  • Meet with your manager to discuss success metrics
  • Ask specifically: "What does success look like in my first 90 days?"
  • Understand your team's key initiatives and priorities
  • Identify any critical upcoming deadlines

Days 15-45: The Competence Phase

Goal: Become operationally proficient while building relationships.

Week 3-4: Independent Contribution
  • Start handling assigned transactions/processes independently
  • Request feedback frequently‚Äînot just formally
  • Join team meetings and listen actively
  • Volunteer for small tasks or projects
Week 5-6: Building Relationship Capital
  • Have 1:1 coffee chats with 3-4 colleagues from different functions
  • Join after-work team activities if available
  • Ask colleagues for advice on challenges
  • Offer help to peers when you can
Week 7: Light Project Involvement
  • Join a small team project or initiative
  • Contribute meaningfully, even if in support role
  • Document what you're learning

Days 45-90: The Impact Phase

Goal: Create visible, measurable impact while establishing yourself as reliable.

Week 8-10: Small Wins Strategy
  • Identify 2-3 small improvements in your processes
  • Examples: Reduce time for a task by 15%, catch a recurring error, improve data quality
  • Implement and document results
  • Share results with manager and team
Week 11-12: Lead a Mini-Project
  • Propose a small process improvement or analysis
  • Get approval and timeline
  • Deliver with quality and on-time
  • Present findings to team or stakeholders
Week 13: 90-Day Review & Reflection
  • Prepare for formal 90-day review
  • Document all accomplishments and learnings
  • Discuss next quarter goals with manager
  • Identify skill gaps to address

The Relationship Strategy That Works

In a traditional organization, you can succeed with technical excellence alone. In a GCC with 500-1000+ employees, relationships are infrastructure.

The Key Relationships to Build

1. Your Direct Manager (Critical)

  • Weekly 1:1 meetings (15-30 mins, structured)
  • Come prepared with 2-3 specific questions or updates
  • Share your learning and challenges
  • Ask for feedback and coaching

2. Peer Support Network (Critical)

  • Identify 3-4 peers at similar or slightly higher levels
  • Build genuine friendships based on mutual learning
  • Ask questions and offer help
  • Peers are your best source of practical knowledge

3. Cross-Functional Contacts (Important)

  • Build relationships with people in other functions (FPA, Tech, HR)
  • Understand their perspectives and needs
  • These connections become valuable as you progress

4. Mentors (Important)

  • Identify 1-2 senior professionals 2-3 levels above you
  • Request quarterly coffee chats for guidance
  • Mentors help you navigate culture and accelerate learning

Success Story: The Relationship Builder

I mentored a CA who, in her first month, identified that she was struggling with Excel formulas. Instead of suffering silently, she asked a colleague in FPA for help. This simple ask led to a 6-month mentoring relationship that accelerated her skills dramatically. More importantly, when an FPA role opened 18 months later, this colleague championed her as the perfect candidate. She's now a senior FPA professional earning 40% more than her peer who stayed in operations.

Key lesson: Asking for help isn't weakness‚Äîit's relationship building in disguise.

The High-Impact Strategy

Creating visible impact in your first 90 days accelerates your career trajectory. Here's the formula:

The Three Types of Impact

Type 1: Operational Excellence (Low Risk, High Credibility)

  • Reduce error rates or processing time in your assigned tasks
  • Example: "I reduced invoicing errors by implementing a new verification step, cutting rework by 8%"
  • Impact: Shows you care about quality and efficiency

Type 2: Process Improvement (Medium Risk, Medium Impact)

  • Identify a broken or inefficient process and improve it
  • Example: "I noticed our daily reconciliation takes 4 hours. I created an automated check that flags discrepancies, reducing manual time to 1.5 hours"
  • Impact: Demonstrates problem-solving and initiative

Type 3: Strategic Initiative (Higher Risk, Highest Impact)

  • Contribute meaningfully to a team or organizational initiative
  • Example: "I joined the month-end close optimization project and helped reduce close timeline by 2 days"
  • Impact: Shows you think beyond your role

My recommendation for first 90 days: Aim for 1-2 Type 1 wins + 1 Type 2 win. This combination proves reliability + initiative without overextending yourself.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: The "I Already Know This" Attitude

Some professionals enter GCCs with advanced credentials and come in thinking they understand everything. They don't ask questions. They don't listen. They don't realize that GCC excellence requires a different mindset than traditional finance. Result: They plateau within 12 months.

Antidote: Adopt a "learn mode" for your first quarter regardless of your background.

Mistake #2: The Solo Performer

Working hard alone impresses no one in a GCC. You need visibility and relationships. Professionals who keep their heads down and execute flawlessly often get overlooked for promotions.

Antidote: Balance execution with strategic visibility through relationships and small showcases of impact.

Mistake #3: The Overachiever

Some new hires try to do everything perfectly and take on too much. They burn out by month 4 or create unrealistic expectations for themselves.

Antidote: Focus on 2-3 areas of excellence rather than 10 areas of adequacy.

Mistake #4: The Complainer

"This process is inefficient," "We should do it differently," "In my previous company, we had better systems." These comments in your first 3 months brand you as someone who doesn't get the organization.

Antidote: First understand why things work the way they do before suggesting changes.
